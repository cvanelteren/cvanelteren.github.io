Why are criminals so succesful?

Last updated on Jun 15, 2023 8 min read

TLDR

  • Unraveling Organized Crime: Complexity science offers a lens to understand the intricate dynamics of organized crime.
  • Insightful Model: Explore Perc and colleagues’ model revealing the emergence of criminals and their complex interactions based on various factors.
  • Interactive Applet: Dive into the simulation below and witness the spontaneous emergence of criminal behavior based on cost, decision error, and punishment effectiveness.

Crime pays, and it pays very well. The United Nations Office on Drugs and Crime estimates that transnational criminal organizations generate an astonishing annual revenue of approximately $1.6 trillion, equivalent to Canada’s Gross Domestic Product. It prompts the question: how is it that criminal organizations are so successful?

It is crucial to realize that (organized) crime is nothing new. Crime occurs in wildly different societies across the globe. Furthermore, it can be tracked back far and wide in recorded history making it seem like crime, if you will, seems like a common occurrence in society.

However, the organized crime remains a danger to our societies by affecting our rule of law, social stability, and social safety.

Organized crime sets itself apart from other forms of crime in for criminal organizations that form a persistent group that co-offends over time. These groups are difficult to combat as they often operate in different jurisdictions making it harder for law enforcement to form a joint effort to convict these individuals. The “organized” part forms a direct offensive to the ruling seat of power. It creates a power struggle for control of resources (and cash flow) that, in the extreme case, can lead to increased violence, creating potential effects to the stability of a economy, resulting in wealth disparities, [insert other examples]

Traditionally, crime is studied from an empirical perspective. The field of criminology is concerned with depicting how crime can change as a function of geographic location, demographic constitution, time of living and so on. These approaches produce a highly detailed, often anthropocentric view of the state of crime and its downstream affects in societies. Over time scientists constructed various different theories as to why crime occurs.

One of the most interesting theories, is to consider criminal organization not as a set of bandits, scheming together to produce mayhem. Rather, it is more constructive to consider criminal actors as highly skilled business people. This economic perspective then places the individual at its center where he or she may weigh the cost and benefit of pursuing a criminal career. Importantly, this perspective is suited to use as a base to study the computational conditions under which criminal organization can thrive or falter – and shameless plug is the topic of some of my research.

The field of computational science is still in its early stages, with few scientists attempting to capture the complex nature of human-human interactions in computational models. These models often leave out intricate details that we hold dear to study a reduced problem that hopefully qualitatively capture the dynamics of criminal organizations.

One of the early approaches was performed by Perc and Colleagues that looked at the interaction between criminals, ordinary people, and punishes (law enforcement). The introduction of roles is crucial as criminal organizations exist by virtue of the supply and demand created by non-criminal actors. As such we could see crime in society as a three way interaction between criminals, law enforcement and ordinary people.

Perc and colleagues applied evolutionary game theory to study the relation between law enforcement criminals and law abiding citizens [1]. This approach considers a set of rational agents that consider a set of possible behaviors depending on the behaviors of other agents they interact with. Applied to crime, this entails that potential individual can opt to choose to become criminal, join law enforcement or remain a normal civilian. The decision to choose one of these behaviors is determined by a cost-to-benefit analysis that an agent performs. An individual will can receive reward $\beta$ for performing a criminal act while receiving a potential punishment from other individuals, denoted by $\gamma$, or from law enforcement, denoted by $\alpha$. This can be captured in so-called payoff matrix:

  Ordinary Punisher Criminal
Ordinary 0 \(-\beta\) 0
Punisher \(\beta\) 0 \(\beta - 1\)
Criminal \(-\alpha\) \(\gamma - \alpha\) \(-\alpha\)

Each entry in the table shows what the outcome would be an interaction between two individuals. For example when an ordinary individual interacts with another ordinary individual it will receive 0 payoff, whereas an ordinary individual with a punished will receive $-\beta$ reward. Perc studied the evolution of a system over time as a function of these three ($\beta,\gamma,\alpha$) parameters.

The dynamics of the system evolve by considering that each decision will contain some noise. This noise can be interpreted as the certainty of an individual about their choice. The transition between choosing either strategy $a$ or $b$ will depend on the expected payoff an individual will have about the outcome of a random interaction.

A commonly used choice to model the choice between two policies is to use the logistic function. The logistic function maps the domain to the range (0, 1). The domain is given by the expected payoff shown in the table above.

We can implement the model in python as follows

python code 
    import proplot as plt, cmasher as cmr, pandas as pd,\
           numpy as np, warnings,\
           re
    from scipy import integrate
    from tqdm import tqdm
    from itertools import product
    warnings.simplefilter("ignore")
    
    def update(t: float, x: np.ndarray, k: float, P: np.ndarray) -> np.ndarray:
        dx = np.zeros_like(x)
    
        # Calculate payoff difference for each state transition
        # The update uses a Fermi transition
        for i in range(3):  # 3 states: O, C, P
            for j in range(3):
                payoff_diff = P[j, i] - P[i, j]
                p_gain = 1 / (1 + np.exp(-1/k * payoff_diff))
                p_loss = 1 / (1 + np.exp(1/k * payoff_diff))
                dx[i] += x[j] * x[i] * p_gain - x[i] * x[j] * p_loss
        return dx
    
    def simulate(settings):
        k = settings.get("k", 0.5)
        alpha = settings.get("alpha", 0.5)
    
        x = np.linspace(0.01, 2, 15)
        t_span = settings.get("t_span", (0, 100))
        t = np.linspace(*t_span, 100)
        colors = np.zeros((x.size, x.size, 3))
    
        x0 = settings.get("x0", np.ones(3) / 3)
        df = [{} for _ in range(x.size * x.size)]
        for idx, (gamma, beta) in tqdm(enumerate(product(x, x))):
            P = np.array([
                    [0, -beta, 0],
                    [beta, 0, beta - 1],
                    [-alpha, gamma - alpha, -alpha]
             ])
    
            res = integrate.solve_ivp(update, t_span = t_span,
                              y0 = x0,
                              args = (k, P,),
                              t_eval = t,
                              dense_output = True
                              )
    
            df[idx] = dict(beta = beta,
                       gamma = gamma,
                       k = k,
                       alpha = alpha,
                       y = res.y,
                       )
        return pd.DataFrame(df)

Note that this implementation does not use the nearest neighbor interaction as shown in the applet above. The applet on the top of this post, is the model how Perc and colleagues specified it. Regardless, we can probe the effect of the cost-to-benefit by systematically varying $\beta$ and $\gamma$ and evolving the system over time, and emulate the original results from Perc and colleagues.

Figure 1 illustrates a complex phase diagram that emerges when varying the cost-to-benefit ratio within a population comprising criminals, ordinary people, and punishers. The diagram reveals three distinct regions based on the interactions among these groups. In the orange/green region, all three groups co-exist, indicating a dynamic balance where each type maintains a presence. The purple area shows a scenario where only criminals and punishers remain, suggesting that ordinary people are either eliminated or assimilated into the other groups. Finally, the blue region represents a situation dominated solely by criminals, where neither ordinary people nor punishers can sustain their populations. These results are derived under the conditions where both $k$ and $\alpha$ are set to 0.5, highlighting the sensitive dependence of societal composition on the cost-to-benefit parameters.

The co-existent phase reveals the circular nature of the dynamics in which a gain of one population may cause a similar increase in another which then leads to a shift that oscillates over time.

By examining organized crime through an economic and computational lens, we gain crucial insights into its persistence and societal impact. Viewing criminal behavior as a series of rational, cost-benefit driven decisions helps us understand the intricate relationships between criminals, law enforcement, and ordinary citizens. Although these models are simplified, they provide a powerful framework for predicting and potentially mitigating the effects of organized crime. As computational science progresses, it will continue to unravel the complexities of human behavior, enabling the development of more effective strategies to combat crime and foster safer, more stable societies.
